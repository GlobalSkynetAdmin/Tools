⚠ Your Current Problem Words dropping at the start and end of sentences = your noise gate threshold is set too high. The gate is closing between words and cutting the quiet parts of your voice. Every setting below is tuned to fix this while keeping background noise out.
Physical Setup First
SM7B Position
Solo Recording
Mic Distance
2–4 inches from your lips. Close mic technique is everything on the SM7B — it's a dynamic mic that rewards proximity. The closer you are, the more bass you get (proximity effect) and the less room noise gets in.
Why: At 6+ inches the SM7B loses low end and requires more gain, which brings in more noise.
2–4" from lips
Mic Angle
Slightly off-axis — angle the capsule about 15–20° away from directly facing your mouth. Reduces plosives (P and B sounds) and sibilance (S sounds) without killing presence.
Why: Direct on-axis pickup of plosives causes low-frequency thuds that are hard to remove in post.
15–20° off-axis
Pop Filter
If you have one, use it. If not, the off-axis technique above is your substitute. The SM7B's built-in A7WS windscreen helps but a separate filter is better for close work.
USE IT if you have one
Rodecaster Pro 2 — Channel Settings
ℹ How to access these on the Rodecaster Pro 2 Tap the channel fader area for your SM7B channel → tap the settings/processing icon → you'll see tabs for EQ, Compressor, Gate, De-esser, and Noise. Set each one per the values below.
Gain (Preamp)
Set This First
Input Gain
The SM7B is a low-output dynamic mic — it needs a lot of gain. Speak at your normal podcast volume and watch the channel meter. You want peaks hitting around -12dB to -6dB on the channel meter. Adjust the gain knob until you're in that range.
Why: Too little gain = quiet recording that requires heavy boosting in Audition which amplifies noise. Too much = distortion and clipping.
-12 to
-6dB peak on meter
Fader Level
Set the channel fader to unity (0dB / 12 o'clock position). Do your gain adjustment with the preamp gain knob, not the fader.
0 dB unity / 12 o'clock
Noise Gate — Fix the Dropout Problem
Your Main Issue
Gate Threshold
This is the volume level below which the gate cuts audio. If set too high, quiet parts of your voice (sentence starts, trailing ends, softer words) get cut. Set it LOW enough that your quietest speaking voice stays open.
Start here: set to -55dB and test. If background noise comes through, raise to -50dB. Never go above -45dB or you'll get dropouts again.
-55 dB start point
Attack
How fast the gate opens when sound hits the threshold. Too slow = clipped word beginnings (your current problem). Too fast = gate chatters on and off rapidly.
A fast attack (1–3ms) opens instantly when you start speaking — no clipping.
1 ms fast open
Release / Hold
How long the gate stays open after your voice drops below threshold. Too short = gate snaps shut mid-word or between syllables. Too long = background noise bleeds in between sentences.
200–300ms is the sweet spot — holds open long enough for natural speech pacing.
250 ms hold time
Compressor — Even Out Your Voice
Warm & Full
Threshold
The level above which compression kicks in. Set it so compression engages on your normal speaking volume — not just the loud peaks.
-18 dB engage point
Ratio
How aggressively loud sounds are reduced. 3:1 means for every 3dB above threshold, only 1dB comes through. For a warm vocal podcast sound you want moderate compression — not squashed, not untouched.
3:1 moderate
Attack
Slow attack lets the initial transient (the punch at the start of a word) through before compression clamps down. This preserves the natural weight of your voice.
10 ms slow-ish
Release
How fast compression lets go. Too fast = pumping sound. Too slow = voice sounds crushed.
120 ms natural
Makeup Gain
Compression reduces overall volume — makeup gain brings it back up. Adjust until your output meter reads the same as before compression was applied.
+4 dB typical starting point
EQ — Shape the Warm Full Sound
Tone Shaping
High Pass Filter
Cuts everything below a set frequency. Removes desk rumble, HVAC hum, footsteps, and low-frequency room noise that you can't hear clearly but muddies the recording.
Set at 80Hz — cuts the junk without touching your voice's bass body.
80 Hz high pass cut
Low Shelf Boost
Adds warmth and body to your voice. The SM7B already has good low end — a gentle boost here thickens it further for the warm/full target tone.
+2 dB @ 150 Hz
Low-Mid Cut
The 250–400Hz range can make voices sound boxy or muddy if it builds up. A small cut cleans this up without thinning the voice.
-2 dB @ 300 Hz
Presence Boost
The 2–5kHz range is where vocal intelligibility lives — where consonants, clarity, and articulation sit. A gentle boost here makes words land clearly even on small speakers or earbuds.
+2 dB @ 3 kHz
High Shelf Cut
Gentle roll-off above 10kHz removes harshness and sibilance without killing air. Keeps the warm character of the SM7B intact.
-1 dB @ 10 kHz
De-esser & Noise
Cleanup
De-esser
Reduces harsh sibilance — the piercing quality on S, SH, and T sounds. The SM7B is relatively smooth but close-mic recording can still exaggerate sibilance.
Frequency: 6–8kHz. Threshold: start at -20dB, reduce until sibilance softens without lisping.
ON 6–8 kHz
Airmix Noise Reduction
The Rodecaster Pro 2's built-in noise reduction. Set to a moderate level — heavy noise reduction creates an unnatural "underwater" sound. You have good acoustic treatment so you don't need heavy-handed processing.
30–40% moderate
Adobe Audition — Final Chain
📋 Audition Processing Order Matters Apply effects in this order on the voice track: Noise Reduction → EQ → Compression → Limiter. Running them out of order produces worse results. The Rodecaster handles the gate and initial compression — Audition is your final polish pass.
Audition Effect Chain — Manual Override
1. Noise Reduction
Capture a noise print from a silent section at the start of your recording (2-3 seconds of room tone). Apply at 60–70% reduction. Don't go higher — artifacts start appearing above 75%.
60–70% reduction
2. Parametric EQ
Fine-tune what the Rodecaster started. The Audition EQ is more precise. Use it to fix any remaining muddiness or harshness that slipped through. Subtle adjustments only — the heavy lifting is done.
subtle fine-tune only
3. Dynamics Compressor
A light second pass of compression for consistency. Ratio 2:1, threshold around -20dB. This catches anything the Rodecaster's compressor missed — especially helpful if you moved closer or further from the mic during recording.
2:1 light pass
4. Hard Limiter
The final ceiling. Set at -1dB to -0.3dB true peak. This absolutely prevents clipping in the export. Non-negotiable — always the last effect in the chain.
-1 dB true peak ceiling
Target Loudness (Export)
Podcast platforms want -16 LUFS for stereo, -19 LUFS for mono. Use Audition's Match Loudness panel before exporting. Spotify, Apple Podcasts, and most platforms normalize to -14 LUFS on playback — hitting -16 LUFS on export lands you right in the sweet spot.
-16 LUFS stereo export
⚠ Face-to-Face Mic Bleed — Your Biggest Challenge With two SM7Bs 4–6 feet apart facing each other, each mic will pick up the other person's voice at a lower level. This creates a "double voice" effect and phase issues in post. The settings below are specifically tuned to minimize this. The gate is your primary weapon — it must be set to cut between speakers.
Physical Setup — Duo Recording
Room & Mic Positioning
Do This Before Settings
Mic Rejection Angles
The SM7B rejects sound from the sides and rear (it's a cardioid mic). Point each mic slightly away from the other person — not directly at the speaker's mouth of the person across from you. This uses the mic's natural rejection pattern to reduce bleed.
The null point (maximum rejection) is directly to the rear of the SM7B capsule. Position so each mic's rear faces roughly toward the other speaker.

opposite speaker

Your existing hex panels are doing real work here. If you can position so each mic has an acoustic panel directly behind the speaker (absorbing reflections that would otherwise travel to the opposite mic) that reduces bleed further.

Adobe Audition — Duo Post-Processing
📋 Record Each Person on a Separate Track In Audition, import Channel 1 and Channel 2 as separate mono tracks. Process each one individually first, then mix together. Never mix down before processing — you lose control of each voice permanently.
Audition Duo Workflow
1. Noise Reduction
Capture separate noise prints for each track — your background noise floor may differ slightly from your wife's mic position. Apply 60% each.
60% each track
2. DeBleed (if needed)

Use Match Loudness on both tracks before mixing — both voices should hit -16 LUFS individually so they sit at the same perceived volume in the final mix.

Apply the hard limiter at -1dB on the master bus after mixing both voices together.

What Each Thing Actually Does
🚪 Noise Gate
Think of it as a door that only opens when someone knocks loud enough. Threshold is how hard the knock needs to be. Attack is how fast the door swings open. Release/Hold is how long it stays open after the knocking stops.

Your problem: the door was set to need too hard a knock, so the first word of each sentence (which starts quietly before building) wasn't loud enough to open it. Solution: lower the threshold so a quieter knock (quieter voice) still opens the gate.
🗜 Compressor
Compressors even out the volume difference between your loud moments and quiet moments. Without compression, a whisper and a shout can be 20dB apart — unlistenable on earbuds. With compression, they sit within 6dB of each other — comfortable to listen to.

Threshold: how loud before it engages. Ratio: how much it clamps down (3:1 = gentle, 10:1 = aggressive). Attack: how fast it grabs. Release: how fast it lets go. Makeup Gain: turns the overall volume back up after compression has reduced it.
🎛 EQ (Equalizer)
EQ is a tone control that boosts or cuts specific frequency ranges. Your voice is a mix of many frequencies simultaneously — bass body (80–250Hz), muddy mids (250–500Hz), nasal range (500Hz–2kHz), presence and clarity (2–5kHz), air and brightness (5kHz+).

High pass filter: cuts everything below a frequency — removes rumble and hum. Shelf: gradually boosts or cuts an entire range above or below a point. Bell/Peak: boosts or cuts a specific narrow frequency range.
🔇 De-esser
A frequency-specific compressor that only activates on harsh S, SH, and T sounds. Those consonants live in the 6–8kHz range. When they peak, the de-esser clamps down only on that frequency range — leaving the rest of your voice untouched. The result is smoother sibilance without losing vocal presence.
📊 LUFS — Loudness Units
LUFS is how loudness is measured for streaming and podcasting. Unlike dB which measures peak levels, LUFS measures perceived loudness over time — which is what actually matters for listener experience.

-16 LUFS: standard podcast target. -14 LUFS: Spotify's normalization point. -23 LUFS: broadcast TV standard (much quieter). Hitting -16 LUFS means your show sounds consistent with professional podcasts on every platform.
🎙 The SM7B + Gain
The SM7B is a dynamic microphone with low output sensitivity. It needs significantly more gain than a condenser mic — typically 60dB or more of clean preamp gain. The Rodecaster Pro 2 can provide this cleanly.

Proximity effect: the closer you are to a dynamic mic, the more bass it picks up. This is a feature, not a bug — it's why close-mic technique on the SM7B sounds warm and full. Pull back and it sounds thinner and requires more gain (which adds noise).
🔄 Signal Chain Order
The order effects are applied matters. Standard podcast chain:

1. Gain/Preamp — set input level correctly
2. High Pass Filter / EQ — remove unwanted frequencies before compressing them
3. Gate — cut silence and bleed
4. Compression — even out dynamics
5. De-esser — tame sibilance after compression
6. Final EQ — fine-tune tone
7. Limiter — hard ceiling, always last
⚡ Quick Troubleshooting
Words dropping at start of sentences → Gate threshold too high or attack too slow. Lower threshold, set attack to 1ms.

Words trailing off at end → Gate release too short. Increase hold time to 250ms.

Voice sounds thin or distant → Move closer to mic. Check high pass filter isn't set too high (above 120Hz).

Voice sounds muddy or boxy → Cut 250–400Hz range by 2–3dB.

Harsh S sounds → De-esser threshold too high or not engaged. Check 6–8kHz de-esser.

Background noise still audible → Audition noise reduction — capture a better noise print from a longer silent section.

Other person's voice bleeding into your track → Raise gate threshold, check mic null point positioning.

Volume inconsistent between episodes → Use Audition Match Loudness panel. Target -16 LUFS every time.
